## Theory

Every first-year EE student can tell you why the NAND gate is the most versatile. It can be used to generate any type of gate (AND/OR/XNOR, etc.) and has the best performance over its counterparts.

Little do they know, the NAND gate isn't just versatile in the digital world — it can even be used in the analog world. Let's take a closer look at the internal structure of the NAND3:

<img src={nand3Transistor} alt="NAND3 transistor level" className="rounded-xl my-6 border border-border-light dark:border-border" />

Nothing too special on its own, but what happens when you pair it precisely with another NAND3 gate in a feedback pattern similar to an SR latch?

<img src={nand3Comparator} alt="Two NAND3 gates configured as an analog comparator" className="rounded-xl my-6 border border-border-light dark:border-border" />

You actually end up with a simple form of clocked analog [comparator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparator)! These devices can actually be used to compare two analog values at the edge of a clock. Furthermore, 7410 chips usually have buffers at their inputs/outputs and for some models they are actually made from a combination of smaller 2-input gates. Even with these issues the comparator still functions (though with larger hysteresis and a smaller dynamic range).

In order to compare continuously, this comparator needs to be followed by a latch or flip flop to keep the value of the last conversion as the comparator is being reset. This can again be made entirely out of NAND3 gates but for simplicity I used a 74175 quad FF chip.

## Measurement Results

In order to prove this theory I took a 7410 triple 3-input NAND chip and built the comparator circuit above. The final circuit I ended up testing is below.

<img src={comparatorFF} alt="Schematic of analog comparator made from 7400 logic chips" className="rounded-xl my-6 border border-border-light dark:border-border" />

The output value needs to be saved before the comparator is reset every cycle. Because the comparator resets at each negative clock edge I used the extra NAND3 gate as an inverter to drive the clock of a 74175 Flip-Flop, capturing the output of the comparator before reset.

With this configuration I tested the comparator at a couple different clock and input frequencies with a fixed comparison level of 2V DC.

Under basic operating conditions (200kHz clock, 100kHz input) the comparator works very well, drawing about 46mA at 5V. The maximum clock frequency I was able to get the comparator to operate correctly was just over **40MHz**. With the 40MHz clock, the highest input frequency that showed anything reasonable topped out at around **4MHz** — which is quite competitive compared to the bandwidth of popular analog comparators like the [LM339](https://www.ti.com/product/LM339).

## Uses

- **Analog Comparator** — Compare two analog voltages and output a logic level.
- **Analog to Digital Converter** — Flash ADCs use banks of comparators; this forms one stage.
- **Servo Driver** — Input a sine wave at the right frequency to generate a slow PWM wave.
- **Sensors** — Temperature, light, sound, and other sensors can be interfaced easily.
- **Level Shifters** — Set the threshold to pass data between different logic voltage domains.

## Future Work

Not only can these NAND gates be used as analog comparators, they can even potentially be used as diodes, variable resistors, and variable capacitors. This opens the door for even more applications.

> NAND gate-based comparators have been shown to work in custom ASICs and have been used to make [Stochastic Flash ADCs](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/search/srchabstract.jsp?arnumber=5986446). The 7410's exact structure (including its input/output buffers) changes the characteristics vs. a pure gate-level model.
